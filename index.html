03 · Simulation

Push the plunger. Watch the particles.

Volume: 100% · Particles per space: 1.0×
Drag the red plunger to the right. The same amount of air is squeezed into less space — that's compression.

What's happening?

Air is made of tiny particles with empty space between them. When you push the plunger, the particles are forced closer together. The amount of air doesn't change — only the space it takes up.

  • Same number of particles
  • Smaller volume
  • Particles push back harder — that's air pressure
04 · Virtual Investigation

Where do we use compressed air?

Bicycle Pump

Pushing the handle down forces air from the pump chamber into the tyre. Inside the tyre, air particles are squeezed together — the tyre becomes firm and can carry your weight.

Try this: Feel the handle become harder to push near the bottom. That's the compressed air pushing back.

Balloon

Blowing into a balloon forces more air particles into a small rubber space. The particles push outward on the balloon walls — that's why it feels tight and springs back when squeezed.

Syringe (with the tip blocked)

With your finger over the tip, pushing the plunger squashes the trapped air. Release the plunger — the compressed air springs the plunger back out!

Football Pump

Same idea as a bicycle pump, but designed to push air through a needle into a football's air valve. The trapped air gives the ball its bounce.

07 · Teacher Guide

For the educator

Learning Intentions
  • Students describe air as matter that occupies space.
  • Students demonstrate compression using models.
  • Students transfer the concept to novel contexts.
Prior Knowledge

P3 Diversity — properties of materials. Students should know that matter takes up space and has mass.

Common Misconceptions
  • "Air is nothing." → Air is matter made of particles.
  • "Compressing air destroys some of it." → No particles are lost; they are pushed closer.
  • "Solids and liquids can also be compressed easily." → They cannot be easily compressed because particles are already close together.
Differentiation

Support: Provide sentence starters, use paired talk, print the particle diagram.

Stretch: Ask students to relate compression to scuba tanks, spray cans, and car airbags.

Assessment Suggestions
  • Exit ticket: Draw before/after particle diagrams.
  • Quiz score in this activity (auto-graded above).
  • POE responses in the Engage section.
Extension Activities
  • Design a poster: "Compressed air in my kitchen."
  • Investigate: Can water be compressed the same way?
  • Research: How divers use compressed air safely.
